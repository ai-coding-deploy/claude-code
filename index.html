Welcome from the Dean

Welcome
from the Dean

Most writing about artificial intelligence is either breathless or bleak. We wanted a third option — a calm, careful place to learn how these tools actually fit into the work most of us already do. CortexLens School was built for the analyst with thirty open tabs, the marketing lead with a Monday brief, the engineer who would rather understand the model than market it.

You will not find shortcuts here, and you will not find anything to buy. You will find twelve plain-spoken courses, written by people who teach for a living and use AI on real projects. Read them in order, read them in any order, or read only the lesson you need before lunch.

We hope you stay a while.

— Dr. Helen Marsh, Dean of Studies

A lesson, end to end

What a CortexLens lesson looks like.

Each lesson is about twelve minutes of reading, one short video walk-through, and one practice exercise you can finish during a coffee break. Here is the shape of every one of them.

  1. A one-paragraph framing of the problem you are trying to solve.
  2. A short video — usually four to six minutes — with on-screen examples.
  3. The core lesson in plain prose, with annotated screenshots.
  4. A practice exercise you can do at your own desk, with no setup.
  5. A short list of common mistakes and how to recognize them in yourself.
Practice exercise · sample

Take the last email you sent that was longer than four paragraphs. Ask an AI assistant to compress it to three short sentences. Then re-send the original. Notice which version actually moved your work forward, and write down why.

Eight readings we keep returning to.

  • A patient, technically honest book about why models behave the way they do. It is the closest thing we have to a textbook for non-engineers, and we assign two chapters in our first course.

  • A short, precise essay on why language models confabulate, written by a researcher who has spent a decade studying the problem. We return to it whenever someone asks "can I trust this answer?"

  • An argument for slowing down with AI output rather than speeding up. Quietly the most influential piece in our verification curriculum, and the one most students email us about.

  • A peer-reviewed study of how professional editors use AI to draft and revise. The methodology section alone is worth the read for anyone designing an AI policy at work.

  • A practical, almost cookbook-like collection of prompting patterns, grouped by task rather than tool. We assign chapters three and seven in the foundations course.

  • Not strictly about AI, but the best book we know on the emotional weather of adopting new software inside a team. Managers in our program reach for it constantly.

  • A clear-headed paper on authorship, consent, and credit when AI assists with writing. It frames the conversations we have in the last lesson of the communications course.

Frequently Asked

Honest answers to honest questions.

Is this really free?

Yes. CortexLens School is informational and free to read. We do not charge for courses, we do not run ads, and we do not resell your data. The school is supported by a small endowment and run as a non-commercial program.

Do I need any background?

None at all. The courses assume you are comfortable with email, a web browser, and the ordinary tools of office work. If you can write a calendar invite, you have enough to begin.

How long does the program take?

Most learners read one lesson a day for about six weeks. There is no schedule, no enrollment, and no consequence for stopping and starting. Some students return only when they need a particular topic.

Do you offer a certificate?

We issue a free completion note — a one-page PDF that acknowledges you finished all twelve courses. It is not an accredited credential and it is not a paid certificate. It is a small, honest record, nothing more.

What tools do you teach?

We teach the patterns of working with general-purpose AI assistants — chat models, writing helpers, spreadsheet copilots. The lessons stay tool-agnostic where possible, with examples drawn from the assistants most readers already use.

Who is behind CortexLens School?

A small team based in Cambridge, Massachusetts — three instructors, one editor, two designers, and an advisory board of faculty from area universities. Mailing address, phone, and email are listed in the footer.
